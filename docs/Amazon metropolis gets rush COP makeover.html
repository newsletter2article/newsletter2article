A rush makeover 

By Peter Millard

Just a few hundred feet from the Amazon River delta, Ivan Ferreira is converting used shipping containers into hotel rooms ahead of the world’s biggest climate conference.

Nearby, a developer is rehabbing a 17-story former office tower into a luxury hotel with more than 170 rooms. Twenty miles outside of Belem, a pier for cruise liners is getting built on an economically depressed island. 

Belem’s role as improbable host of the November summit has spawned opportunities and challenges for the city of 1.3 million in northern Brazil, just below the equator. It’s going through its biggest surge of urban renewal since a rubber boom turned it into “the Metropolis of the Amazon” more than a century ago. Some worry that the rush will end in logistical chaos.

Unlike past COP venues such as Dubai and Glasgow, Scotland, the city lacks an established tourism sector. Despite its prized location near the mouth of the Amazon, world-class cuisine and historic architecture, it doesn’t draw many international visitors.

Aerial view of Parque da Cidade, the main venue for COP30, under construction in Belém, Brazil, in early May 2025. Photographer: Alessandro Falco/Bloomberg

Belem also has some of Brazil’s worst poverty and patchy public services. According to official statistics, 80% of residents aren’t connected to the municipal sanitation system. Twelve percent don’t get their trash collected and around 4% have no running water. 

This is part of the reason why President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva chose Belem to hold COP30 in the first place. There’s potent symbolism in holding a COP in the Amazon, since the rainforest is critical to the global fight against climate change. But Lula also wants to show the hardships of the millions of people who live there, to drive home his twin goals of environmental conservation and raising living standards.

It won’t be easy. To meet the demand for accommodation, authorities are upgrading schools and army barracks where beds will be set up. They’ve even reached out to religious seminaries.

Real estate agent Wal Azevedo says all you need to do is give her a call. She expects a flood of apartments to become available in the runup to the event because many residents will be heading out of town. The state government has declared a school holiday and public servants will be encouraged to work from home.

Azevedo is offering apartments for between $200 and $600 a night per room, and is renting her own home to a group of 10 people for $4,000 a night. She is still figuring out where she’ll stay during the conference, she said: “I have a Plan A, a Plan B and a Plan C.”

More from Green

The Federal Reserve has disbanded a number of internal groups set up to help the US central bank identify and respond to financial stability threats posed by climate change.

Among those dismantled are the Supervision Climate Committee and the Financial Stability Climate Committee, which were established in early 2021. That’s around the time the Fed — under President Joe Biden — began to speak more openly about the financial implications of a hotter planet and increasingly erratic weather patterns. Former Fed Vice Chair Lael Brainard said at the time that the two committees were part of an effort to build “institutional capacity and knowledge” on climate risks and vulnerabilities. 

Both committees were shut down in March, according to a person familiar with the matter who asked not to be identified disclosing confidential information. Two other climate-focused groups — the Climate Committee on Economic Activity and the Climate Data Committee — were also shuttered around that time, the person said. 

Fed Chair Jerome Powell has acknowledged the threats that climate change poses, but said the central bank doesn’t have a mandate to foster a low-carbon transition. Photographer: Tierney L. Cross/Bloomberg

The Trump Administration terminated a $3 billion loan guarantee awarded to Sunnova Energy Intentional Inc. after the solar company told officials it no longer intended to tap into the facility.

The European Union’s progress toward its climate targets risks being impaired by its latest guidance on carbon-removal credits, according to German research nonprofit Oeko-Institut.

Saudi Arabia said it’s stepping up efforts to curb unauthorized pilgrims from the annual Hajj journey that starts next week, a year after more than 1,300 people died during the gathering amid extreme heat.

Japan is hoping local startups can help the nation prepare for and adapt to a warmer world. The government is offering ¥60 billion ($415 million) of support for the commercialization of homegrown climate and environmental technology.
