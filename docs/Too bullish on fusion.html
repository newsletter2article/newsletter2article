Any day now

By Annie Massa and Will Wade

There’s a hackneyed quip about fusion power: Taming the power of the stars is 30 years away, and it always will be. But lately, a rueful twist has emerged. Now that billionaire investors and VCs are putting big money into the problem, the ultimate clean-energy technology is just five years away — and always will be!

That’s raising key questions about the companies working on the problem. When startups set their own deadlines, how likely are they to actually meet them? And is there a possibility that some might be overly optimistic, to win support from investors?

Even with support from deep-pocketed backers including Jeff Bezos, George Soros and Bill Gates, companies working to deliver fusion power require patience. The engineering and physics challenges are daunting — the technology only generated net energy for the first time in 2022 — and the industry is well-known for blowing past deadlines. 

But there are causes for optimism. Besides the 2022 breakthrough, achieved at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, dozens of startups are racing to commercialize fusion. Most of them expect to reach that goal sometime between 2030 and 2035, according to the Fusion Industry Association’s 2024 annual report issued this month.

A handful even expect to do so before the end of this decade, notably Helion Energy Inc. The company ranks among the industry’s best-funded ventures, having raised more than $600 million, according to PitchBook. It’s done so largely thanks to billionaire investor and chairman (and OpenAI Chief Executive Officer) Sam Altman, who has heavily backed the company along with venture capitalist Peter Thiel’s Mithril Capital and Meta Platforms Inc. co-founder Dustin Moskovitz.

Helion Energy's facility in Everett, Washington. Photographer: Mike Kane/Bloomberg

Helion agreed last year to provide Microsoft Corp. with electricity from a fusion power plant, which it says it can build by 2028. Of the companies aiming to deliver fusion power this decade, only the Everett, Washington-based startup has been so open about its plans, said Andrew Holland, the Fusion Industry Association’s chief executive of

“That’s as hard a deadline as you can get,” he said. “They’re the only ones that are public and out there.”

Yet some scientists are skeptical Helion can meet that timeline, in part because the company has been less open than others about its technical progress. Helion hasn’t published peer-reviewed experimental results the way its largest rivals do. The company says its main goal is building a fusion power plant, not necessarily publishing.

“When we founded Helion, our goal wasn’t to demonstrate interesting new physics,” CEO David Kirtley said in an interview in March. “By iterating and building quickly, you actually learn more than you ever could if you did a lot of simulation and a lot of computation upfront.”

But at least some in the scientific community see that approach as a red flag. What Helion has done is set targets, and researchers will soon find out if the company reaches them.

The Lawrence Livermore milestone is one commercial companies have been racing to emulate, similar to what Helion in 2021 said it would reach this year, though using a different approach. When it announced the Microsoft deal last year, the company said it was building its seventh prototype, which would “demonstrate the ability to produce electricity in 2024.” The year isn’t complete, but the industry is still waiting. 

“They say they’ll publish when they’ve accomplished something,” said Holland, who expects the company to meet its 2028 target. 

The promise of the technology Helion and other companies are working on is immense. Conventional nuclear plants use fission, splitting large atoms in a process that generates energy. Fusion is the opposite, fusing smaller atoms together to release energy. That will require controlling reactions at more than 100 million degrees Celsius.

It’s not a small feat, but the technology offers the promise of abundant, carbon-free power. The longer it takes the world to meaningfully curb carbon emissions, the more urgent that need becomes. But it can’t wait indefinitely for fusion’s moment to arise.

More from Green

China’s electric carmakers are expanding in Europe to blunt the impact of tariffs meant to weaken their price advantage over the region’s ailing legacy manufacturers.

With the European Union hiking duties on Chinese electric vehicles to as much as 48%, China’s new generation of green car manufacturers is teaming up with local industry so their cars are considered homegrown. Without these measures, Chinese EVs could become thousands of euros more expensive for consumers, or else unprofitable. 

The arrival of China’s EV makers is a risk for European auto giants, which have little choice but to strike partnerships and make space for their upstart rivals as they face shuttering some of their own sites to adjust to faltering global sales growth. 

Geely’s Zeekr electric vehicles bound for shipment to Europe at the Port of Taicang in China in 2023. The firm is looking to shift some production to European factories. Source: Bloomberg

Weather watch

By Eamon Farhat and Paul Tugwell

Heat and wildfires are searing the Mediterranean, while this evening’s opening ceremony for the Paris Olympics will be dampened by rain.

Temperatures in Spain are again set to breach 40C (104F) in the interior on Friday, after the country’s high for this year of 45.6C was recorded in Huelva on Wednesday.

Police boats patrol the River Seine ahead of the Olympic Games in central Paris. Photographer: Nathan Laine/Bloomberg

In Canada, rainfall is expected to bring some relief to a fast-moving wildfire that ripped through the Rocky Mountain resort town of Jasper, destroying homes and businesses and threatening key infrastructure in the evacuated Alberta community.

The area saw cooler temperatures and 10 to 15mm (0.6 inches) of rainfall shortly after midnight local time, Jasper National Park said in an update on X, formerly Twitter. That should help keep fire activity low for the next 72 hours.
