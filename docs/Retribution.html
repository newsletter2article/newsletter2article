Retribution

The Collapse Chronicle
Mar 21
 
READ IN APP
 

In 1973, the Justice department brought a suit against Fred and Donald Trump and their real estate company, in 1973 for violations of the Fair Housing Act of 1968 in their many complexes--39 buildings, between them containing over 14,000 apartments. The Trumps were famously defended by Roy Cohn. After two years, the matter settled with a consent decree, signed in 1975 which Justice Department called “one of the most far-reaching ever negotiated.” And thus was at least one set of grievances born.

During the past campaign season, the current occupant of the Oval Office said: “I am your warrior. I am your justice. For those who have been wronged and betrayed, I am your retribution.” We have begun to see what that looks like and the forms that retribution will take.

He has removed security details for people who have displeased him, including General Mark Milley, former Secretary of State Mike Pompeo, former National Security Adviser John Bolton, and the infectious-disease expert Anthony Fauci, as well as adult children of the Biden family.

He has called CNN and MSNBC “corrupt” and “illegal”—not because of breaking laws, but simply because they have been insufficiently adoring or submissive.

He has targeted law firms and targeted a federal judge, James E. Boasberg, who ordered a pause in deportations being carried out under an obscure law from 1798.

In a recent speech at Justice, the president described his adversaries as “scum,” “savages,” and “Marxists,” as well as “deranged,” “thugs,” “violent vicious lawyers,” and “a corrupt group of hacks and radicals within the ranks of the American government.” It's clear what that means: The Justice Department is Trump’s weapon for revenge. And his appetite for vengeance is bottomless. And no action intended to make the world safe for mediocre white men is too small to be considered.

This week we had the opportunity to see that play out in an AI-fueled purge of Department of Defense websites. At least the PR flacks burbling "clarifications" were blaming the deletions on AI.

Perhaps the most recognizable deletion was that of Jackie Robinson, the man who broke the all-white color line in Major League baseball. During the war, Robinson served honorably in the US Army. 14 of the 18 articles about Robinson's military service were deleted.

Content about indigenous Navajo code talkers was deleted.

Other articles deleted in the DOD DEI purge:

-The 54th Massachusetts Regiment at Fort Wagner during the American Civil War

-Charles C. Rogers, the highest-ranking black officer to be awarded the Medal of Honor

-Colin Powell, the first African American chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, were deleted.

-Indigenous women who have served in the U.S. armed services

-The Tuskegee Airmen

-A profile of Ira Hayes, a native American who was among those raising the American flag at Iwo Jima, was deleted.

The military’s official line is, “Content is being reviewed and removed to align with the President's executive orders and DoD priorities in accordance with DoD instruction 5400.17.” But after several days of public uproar and revulsion, the pages about Jackie Robinson have been restored.
